The Committee & the Market

Maine Democrats must name a Senate nominee by July 27 — no primary runoff, a party committee decides — and prediction markets price every branch of the choice. Their verdict: the longshots are confidently priced out, but the live question — Jackson or Bellows — sits inside the error bars, and cheap capital alone can't buy the answer. The pre-registered claims — and the prices they rest on — were locked before the decision; live prices update on top, the record underneath never moves. So "the market knew" gets graded, not asserted.

The capital tax

There is a second force widening these bands beyond trading economics (the bands themselves are derived in the footnotes), and it is a pure market-design artifact. The nine conditions are mutually exclusive — exactly one candidate gets the nomination, so at most one answer can ever cost a multi-answer bettor money. But Manifold margins every answer independently: full stake posted on every leg, even the ones that are guaranteed refunds.

The cost depends on the shape of the book. A conviction-weighted book over the live candidates posts about 1.6–2× its worst-case loss. The pairwise ranking trade — YES on one conditional, NO on another, the trade that answers the committee's actual question — posts exactly 2× (only one leg can ever lose). A uniform market-making quote across the whole board — the participant a decision market needs most — pays the full 9×. The deepest tax lands on the most useful participants; the only untaxed shape is the single-leg directional bet, which carries the least decision information.

Worst-case-loss margining (net capital = the maximum loss over the exclusive branches) deletes the (1−p_B)/p_B term from the band formula for any trader running a book across answers: idle legs would no longer tie capital, so only the live branch's c·r·T_live remains. On today's board:

Design footnote: the refund machinery is already per-answer (we verified the resolution code), so netting is an accounting change, not a resolution change. The missing piece is that in independent-answer markets Manifold has no way to know the conditions are exclusive — it would take a creator-declared "exclusive conditions" flag, with the margin relief scoped to it. That flag is arguably the single cheapest infrastructure change that would make platform decision markets work.

The price of precision

Suppose someone — realistically, Manifold itself, with house money — wanted to make this board sharper before July 27. There are two levers, and they are orthogonal:

Reforms lower the floors. The bands are incentive floors set by trading economics; no amount of liquidity moves them. Netting (above) deletes the conditional-specific penalty. Cheaper carry — patient capital that prices locked mana at, say, 0.5%/month instead of 2% — scales every floor down linearly.

Subsidy pays someone to reach the floors — and for patient capital, subsidy is the floor. The carry floor is a marginal condition (edge per mana vs. interest per mana), so pool depth cancels out of it — but the total prize for correcting a mispricing ε scales with depth, and a trader with fixed attention cost C participates only if the prize covers it. That gives a second floor, ε ≈ √(2cC/pBD), which tightens as 1/√depth. Calibration: correcting a band-edge error on the Jackson conditional pays about M$1 today — this is a market that pays one mana for a five-point insight. (Not a coincidence, a measurement: at C ≈ M$1 the attention floor and the carry floor coincide at today's depth.) The min-bet granularity floor, for comparison, is ~0.15 points here — subsidy solved that constraint at the first M$100; rates, not tick size, set today's bands.

Which floor binds depends on who sets marginal prices. For capital-constrained traders (mana earning 2%/month elsewhere) carry binds and subsidy is nearly futile. For patient capital — idle mana, hobby attention, r ≈ 0 — only the attention floor binds, and there subsidy is the whole game. The bet-feed audit above is evidence for the patient regime: the tail answers trade at all, at prices far inside any carry band. And in that regime the arithmetic turns sharp: M$10k per answer drops the attention floor to about ±1.3 points per side — 2.6 combined, under the 3-point Jackson–Bellows gap. If the patient-capital reading is right, subsidy alone can buy the ranking; the ladder below is the pessimistic case.

The anatomy of a band

Each published band is a sum of floors, and the floors respond to different levers. The tick floor (min-bet granularity, ~0.2 points here) was solved by the first M$100 of liquidity. The carry floor splits into two branches: capital locked until November if the candidate is picked, and capital parked until July 27 in the (likely, for tail candidates) refund branch — the slider above moves both. The attention floor is what's left when capital is free: someone still has to notice, and the prize must cover the noticing.

The loans toggle exists because Manifold's leverage system changes the carry arithmetic more than any plausible disagreement about r. A position holder can reclaim 1% of position value per day as an interest-free loan (linear — a position fully recycles in ~100 days; paid members 2–3%/day), and we verified against the resolution source that this is safe even on the refund branch: the loan is repaid by deduction from the N/A refund, so the holder stays whole in every world and meanwhile had the capital back. Loans shrink the four-month live branch by ~2.4× and the two-week refund branch by only ~7% — they tighten exactly the bands that were already the informative ones. The platform's margin-loan rate (0.03%/day ≈ 0.9%/mo) is also the honest anchor for r itself: nobody rational carries at 5%/mo while the house lends at 0.9%. Fine print: loan capacity per answer caps at 5% of a trader's equity, so the no-loans column is still the right model for a single whale trying to buy a correction alone.

The other structure added Jul 11

The capital tax and the carry floor are properties of the conditional-board structure, not of prediction markets. So we built the structure that dodges the tax, within current platform rules: price the joints — "Jackson defeats Collins," not "Jackson wins if nominated" — as one mutually-exclusive market. Exclusivity is then enforced by the market mechanism itself (complete sets of shares redeem to cash automatically), so a trader quoting the whole board, or trading the ranking, posts roughly worst-case loss instead of every leg. The catch, and the reason the board above stays the instrument of record: joint prices for long-shot nominees live below Manifold's 1% price floor, where the market literally cannot quote them. The board's refund structure is what keeps every candidate readable — the carry its bands price in is the fee for that. Two structures, two halves of the problem. This race now has both, and they grade each other:

Hanson's objections, graded on live data

partly refuted

"Conditional markets are too thin to mean anything"

For the two live front-runners the bands are ±5–6 points on M$600-ish pools — genuinely informative. The objection survives only in the tail (±16 to ±50 points) and for slow-resolving conditions. Thinness is a function of pB and the decision clock, not a blanket property.

confirmed

"It can't actually rank the options"

Jackson leads Bellows by 3 points; both bands are ±5–6. The market prices each candidate well and their difference not at all. It prunes tails loudly (not Platner: 0.137; probably not Mills: 0.418) and shrugs at the actual choice the committee faces.

confirmed

"Conditional prices are confounded — evidential, not causal"

These prices are P(win | the party chose X), not P(win | do(nominate X)). Mills' 0.418 is the poster child: the worlds where a committee reaches past two front-runners for the ex-governor are worlds where something else went wrong. The market's most contrarian claim is also its most confounded.

refuted mechanically

"Arbitrage will keep conditionals honest"

Riskless arbitrage is structurally repelled: the N/A refund branch breaks every static payout table (our own arb bot disabled its Maine conditional legs for exactly this). Only opinionated capital disciplines a conditional — unless you hedge with the conditioner market, which restores the joint at exactly c·p and is how the family's consistency law (footnotes) becomes tradeable at all.

wrong objective

"Who chose the question?"

The markets condition on a Democrat winning. The anti-Collins coalition's objective is Collins losing — in an RCV state that elected Angus King, those differ in every strong-independent world. The party market prices "Other" at 0.000: door closed, by assumption. Whoever writes the market writes the objective function.

untested here

"If the decision follows the market, the market breaks"

The committee never committed to follow these prices, so the self-reference regime — manipulation, self-fulfilling equilibria, he-who-sets-incentives — stays untested. With ~64 traders and M$3,000 of subsidy, a committed decision rule would be trivially cheap to lean on. This is the experiment nobody has run.

Footnotes

The "min worthwhile trade" knob

One knob, two floors. Manifold's literal minimum bet is M$1, but the effective minimum is whatever participants consider worth the bother — nobody logs in to place a one-mana correction. The knob sets both at once: the price-granularity unit (a market where nobody trades under M$m moves in M$m-impact steps — linear in m) and the noticing prize (a mispricing gets corrected only if the prize covers at least one trade worth making — the attention floor, scaling as √m). The M$1 default matches the C ≈ M$1 calibration measured on this board, so the frozen record is the untouched setting. Stake floor and prize floor are conflated deliberately; they are the same order of magnitude here. Note what lowers m in the real world: better UI. That is the quiet argument for a purpose-built conditional-markets viewer.

Where the bands come from (the derivation)

A conditional market that refunds on N/A has a peculiar incentive structure, and it is computable. A trader who thinks the price is off by ε and bets faces two futures:

BranchProbabilityWhat happensCapital locked
Candidate not picked1 − pBfull refund (verified against Manifold source)~2 weeks — until Jul 27
Candidate pickedpBbet rides; edge ε pays off~4 months — until Nov 3

Setting expected profit against the cost of locked capital (rate r) gives the no-correction band — prices inside it are invisible to rational money:

ε* ≈ c · r · ( Tlive + TN/A · (1−pB)/pB ) + tick(pool)

Three things fall out. First, conditionality is nearly free for front-runners: because the N/A branch resolves in two weeks, the conditional-specific penalty (1−p_B)/p_B·T_NA only explodes below pB ≈ 0.15. The folk objection "thin conditional markets can't price anything" is really about longshot conditions with slow resolution. Second, the fee-free minimum bet (M$1 moves these pools ~0.1–0.2 points) means carry is the entire floor — the bands on the board are the market's resolution. Third, a band is a statement about incentives, not freshness: prices can also simply be old. That failure mode needs its own check (we ran it — the bet-feed audit under the board; this market passes), and a price's evidential value decays with world-change since its last meaningful trade, not with today's pB. Sensitivity: ε* is linear in r; we quote r = 2%/month — halve or 2.5× the bands for 1% or 5%, or move the slider above the board.

The loans extension (added 2026-07-11, before publication; the frozen bands remain the no-loan case). Manifold's daily free loan returns ρ = 1% of a position's value per day, linearly, interest-free, repaid by deduction at resolution — including N/A resolution, which is why it is safe on the refund branch (verified against the resolution source: the deduction extinguishes the loan, the holder keeps the loan cash, net whole in every world). Locked capital at day t is then max(0, 1 − ρt) of the stake, so a horizon of T days costs only

E(T) = T − ρT²/2 (for T ≤ 1/ρ; capped at 1/2ρ = 50 locked-day equivalents)

and the band becomes ε* ≈ c · r · ( E(Tlive) + E(TN/A) · (1−pB)/pB ). Two consequences: no Manifold position ever costs more than ~100 locked-day equivalents of carry, whatever its horizon; and because E compresses long horizons and spares short ones, loans shrink the live branch (×0.42 at four months) far more than the two-week refund branch (×0.93) — the front-runners' bands tighten ~2.2×, the tails' barely move. Caps (5% of equity per answer, 10%/day portfolio-wide) mean this discount belongs to diversified capital, not to a single concentrated corrector.

Does the family even add up? (yes — to within about a point)

Decision markets come with a consistency law: P(Dem win) = Σ P(nominee=X)·P(win|X). Multiply each row of the board and stack the products; the total should land on the directly-traded P(Dem win). It does.

Stacked: nominee-probability × conditional, per candidate; hatched = the 0.057 of nominee mass with no conditional priced (shown at 0.40–0.55, midpoint drawn). Ticks: the three directly-traded P(Dem win) prices. Sum ≈ 0.583–0.592 vs. direct 0.578 / 0.584 / 0.596.

The Republican side is equally tidy: Collins-wins-reelection trades at 0.391 against party-Republican at 0.423, and the 0.032 wedge is separately priced — a "Collins clears 95% in her primary" market sits at 0.966, i.e. ~3.4% primary-loss risk. Even the redundancies agree: a standalone "Troy Jackson wins the seat" market trades at 0.357 vs. the board's implied 0.564 × 0.620 = 0.350. Whatever these markets are, they are not internally silly.
