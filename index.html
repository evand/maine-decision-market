The Committee & the Market

By July 27, a committee of Maine Democrats must name a Senate nominee — the primary winner withdrew, there is no runoff, a room decides. Prediction markets price every branch of that choice, and this page is the briefing the committee will never ask for: the market has confidently ruled out Platner — and no one else; on the live question — Jackson or Bellows — it is silent within its own error bars, and making it speak has a price, which we compute below.

The conditional market

Each row is a candidate the committee could pick. The dot is a market price: the probability that a Democrat holds the seat in November if this person is the nominee. These are conditional markets — every bet on a candidate who isn't chosen is refunded, so each row is a clean what-if: bettors risk nothing on the branches the committee doesn't take. A family of markets shaped this way — one price for every branch of a choice someone must actually make — is a decision market, and live ones are rare.

The shaded band is the part most market-reading leaves out. A price only stays correct if correcting it pays, and correcting these pays badly: money sits locked until November if the candidate is picked, and idles until July 27 for a mere refund if not. The band is the computed no-correction band — how far the price can drift before fixing it beats simply holding the money, a waiting cost traders call carry (derivation in the footnotes). Read each row like a poll with a margin of error, except this margin comes from incentives, not sampling.

Can these prices be trusted at all?

Two checks, both passing. Freshness first, because a wide band could simply mean nobody is watching: the Jul-10 bet feed shows every row traded within the prior two days, and the two "dead" candidates repriced hardest after the withdrawal — Platner's row alone saw M$3,001 of trading in the two days after he quit (Jul 8–10). The wide bands are an incentive limit, not neglect: small opinionated money keeps these prices current even where the carry math says it shouldn't bother.

Coherence second. A decision-market family comes with an arithmetic identity: nominee odds × win odds, summed across the candidates, must equal the directly traded P(Dem win). Stack the products and check —

— and, at the frozen 2026-07-10 prices, the family adds up to within a point. The Republican side is equally tidy at the same snapshot: Collins-wins-reelection trades at 0.391 against party-Republican at 0.423, and the 0.032 wedge is separately priced — a "Collins clears 95% in her primary" market sits at 0.966, i.e. ~3.4% primary-loss risk. Even the redundancies agree: a standalone "Troy Jackson wins the seat" market trades at 0.357 vs. the family's implied 0.564 × 0.620 = 0.350. Whatever these markets are, they are not internally silly.

What the committee could read off these prices

Drop Platner; be careful claiming more. He prices at .137, far enough down that his gap clears both rows' floors (±39 combined) at the settings in view. Wood at .400 looks pruned too, but that reading charges only the front-runners' bands: charge his own row as well — the sum rule this page runs on — and the comparison's floor (±36) swallows his 22-point gap; his band overlaps both leaders on the chart above. One ruling-out comes free; the rest of the tail is unmeasured, not condemned.

The Shah row is an open door, not an endorsement. It reads .600 — level with the front-runners — next to nomination odds of 3.7%: a tempting story (“he'd do fine; the committee just won't pick him”). Trust only half of it. The nomination number is the confident half; the conditional is measured to ±37.2 at the settings in view — his band overlaps both leaders on the chart — and, like every row here, it prices P(win | the committee chose him): worlds where the committee had its reasons, and worlds where the front-runners were no longer available. The question “should he be in consideration?” is one no row on this chart answers.

Read the Mills number sideways. Her .418 looks like "weak candidate." Not quite: these prices forecast worlds, not effects of choices. The worlds where a committee reaches past two front-runners for the ex-governor are mostly worlds where something else has already gone wrong. The market's most contrarian number is also its most confounded — a flag, not a verdict.

Do not read Jackson .620 over Bellows .590 as advice. The 3-point gap is inside the pair's combined floor — carry ±5.9 plus attention ±9.3 = ±15.2; a corrector pays to notice on top of paying to wait: nobody is being paid to make that difference meaningful, so it isn't. This is the market's central, least intuitive lesson — it measures each candidate to a few points, and measures the comparison between the top two, the actual decision, not at all.

The price of a sharper answer

Each band is a sum of three floors, and each floor answers to a different lever. The tick floor — the price step from the smallest trade anyone makes, ±0.1 points at the depth in view — is already negligible: the market's first M$100 of depth took care of it. The carry floor: money locked to November if the candidate is picked, money idled to July 27 for a refund if not — both kinds of waiting have to be paid for. The attention floor is what remains when the waiting is free: someone still has to notice the error, and the prize has to cover the noticing — at the settings in view it, not carry, is what binds the front-runners.

Carry, and the refund-branch penalty. For a front-runner, conditionality is nearly free — Jackson's band is almost all ordinary locked-to-November carry. For a longshot it's brutal: a bet on Mills's row idles to July 27 for a refund in 98.5% of worlds, so her carry band balloons to ±28. Manifold's interest-free loans (a holder can reclaim 1% of a position per day, safely even on the refund branch) shrink the live branch (to November) by ~2.4× and the short refund branch by only ~7% — they tighten exactly the rows that were already the informative ones. The loans toggle above shows it; the fine print (rate anchors, per-answer caps, why a lone whale can't use this) is in the derivation footnote.

The every-leg tax. The nine conditions are mutually exclusive — at most one answer can ever cost a multi-answer bettor money — but Manifold margins each answer independently: full stake posted on every leg, including the guaranteed refunds. The trade that answers the committee's actual question (YES on one front-runner, NO on the other) posts exactly 2× its worst-case loss; a market-maker quoting every answer — the participant a decision market needs most — posts 9×. The deepest tax lands on the most useful participants. Margining to worst-case loss instead ("netting") would delete the refund-branch penalty for anyone betting across several answers. The missing piece is that in independent-answer markets Manifold has no way to know the conditions are exclusive — it would take a creator-declared "exclusive conditions" flag, with the margin relief scoped to it. That flag is arguably the single cheapest infrastructure change that would make platform decision markets work.

Subsidy, and whom it helps. Reforms lower the floors; subsidy pays someone to reach them — and which lever matters depends on whose money sets marginal prices. For capital-constrained traders (mana earning 2%/month elsewhere), carry binds and subsidy is nearly futile. For patient capital — idle mana, hobby attention — only the attention floor binds, and that floor tightens with the square root of pool depth: deepen the pools and the same error pays a bigger prize. The freshness audit above is evidence the patient regime is the real one here: the tail answers trade at all, at prices far inside any carry band. Calibration, at the Jul-10 snapshot's depth: correcting a band-edge error on Jackson's row paid about M$1 — a market that pays one mana for a five-point insight.

So the ladder prices out as: the market in view can rule out only part of the tail (even netting doesn't finish it at these settings — patient capital plus subsidy does). Even netting doesn't reach the Mills question at these settings. Jackson-vs-Bellows needs either the full reform stack — or patient capital plus real subsidy (the quadrature arithmetic above). And Shah-vs-Bellows is not a funding problem at all: its gap sits at Manifold's 1% price grid — a tie at the market's working precision, and no depth will pretend otherwise.

The house-money case: reforms take engineering and time this decision doesn't have, but the subsidy lever exists today — and Manifold's own tooling can aim it at single answers (ordinary users can't; at the odds in view, ~78% of a whole-market subsidy would land outside the two live rows). The refund asymmetry keeps the cost low: subsidy parked in answers that end in refunds comes back, in full, on July 27 — the house risks real exposure only on the one answer that survives the decision. Given the prices, liquidity, and assumptions in view, depth is the wrong lever at these settings: the waiting cost alone (±5.9 combined) swallows the 3-point gap, so no pool size pays a corrector who is still paying to wait at r = 2.0%/mo + loans. Cheaper capital — the cost-of-capital slider, loans, or the margin reform above — has to come first; set r to 0 and this paragraph prices the patient-capital case.

Mechanism note: per-answer subsidy targeting is currently internal-only API surface; a pending mechanism upgrade (cpmm-multi-2) would open it to everyone — for a two-answer aim that's ~4.5× more of each subsidized mana landing on target — and would stop the current liquidity-add discarding a small slice of every contribution. Better if merged; nothing above depends on it.

What the theorists would tell the committee

Robin Hanson proposed decision markets — condition a market on each option, let the prices advise the chooser — three decades ago, and the objections have been rehearsed ever since, mostly on whiteboards. This family is a rare live test bench. Six standard objections, graded against it:

partly refuted

"Conditional markets are too thin to mean anything"

For the two live front-runners the bands are ±5–6 points on M$600-ish pools (Jul-10 snapshot) — genuinely informative. The objection survives only in the tail (±16 to ±50 points) and for slow-resolving conditions. Thinness is a function of the nomination odds and the decision clock, not a blanket property.

confirmed

"It can't actually rank the options"

Jackson leads Bellows by 3 points; both bands are ±5–6. The market prices each candidate well and their difference not at all. It prunes the deepest tail loudly (not Platner: 0.137) and shrugs at the actual choice the committee faces.

confirmed

"Conditional prices are confounded — evidential, not causal"

These prices are P(win | the party chose X), not P(win | do(nominate X)). Mills' 0.418 is the poster child: the worlds where a committee reaches past two front-runners for the ex-governor are worlds where something else went wrong. The market's most contrarian claim is also its most confounded.

refuted mechanically

"Arbitrage will keep conditionals honest"

Riskless arbitrage is structurally repelled: the refund branch breaks the fixed payout tables arbitrage bots rely on (our own bot disabled its Maine conditional legs for exactly this). Only opinionated capital disciplines a conditional — unless you hedge with the nominee market, which reconstructs the joint bet at exactly c·p (conditional price × nominee odds) and is how the family's consistency law (the coherence check above) becomes tradeable at all.

Live demonstration, Jul 11 evening: hours after the joint market (the outcome-pair structure introduced below) launched, its Jackson ratio drifted ~10 points off the conditional market — and our automated arbitrage rules stayed (correctly) silent. Only opinionated or refund-aware capital can close it; the S1 tracker below is watching.

wrong objective

"Who chose the question?"

The markets condition on a Democrat winning. The anti-Collins coalition's objective is Collins losing — in a ranked-choice (RCV) state that elected Angus King, those differ in every strong-independent world. The party market prices "Other" at 0.000: door closed, by assumption. Whoever writes the market writes the objective function.

untested here

"If the decision follows the market, the market breaks"

The committee never committed to follow these prices, so the self-reference regime — manipulation, self-fulfilling equilibria, he-who-sets-incentives — stays untested. With ~64 traders and M$3,000 of subsidy (Jul 10), a committed decision rule would be trivially cheap to lean on. This is the experiment nobody has run.

Footnotes

The "min worthwhile trade" knob

One knob, two floors. Manifold's literal minimum bet is M$1, but the effective minimum is whatever participants consider worth the bother — nobody logs in to place a one-mana correction. The knob sets both at once: the price-granularity unit (a market where nobody trades under M$m moves in M$m-impact steps — linear in m) and the noticing prize (a mispricing gets corrected only if the prize covers at least one trade worth making — the attention floor, scaling as √m). The M$1 default matches the C ≈ M$1 calibration measured on the conditional market, so the frozen record is the untouched setting. Stake floor and prize floor are conflated deliberately; they are the same order of magnitude here. Note what lowers m in the real world: better UI. That is the quiet argument for a purpose-built conditional-markets viewer.

How the floors combine over a pair trade (added Jul 11). The ranking question is a pair trade — YES on one conditional, NO on the other — and the two ends of this knob combine differently across its legs. The mechanical end is per-leg: Manifold's API enforces amount ≥ M$1 per bet call (and quantizes resting limit orders to whole percentage points), so tick floors add linearly. The bother end is per-trade: the thinking cost C attaches to the entire trade — "the J–B gap is mispriced" is one insight, the second click is nearly free. The prize for correcting a mispricing ε on leg i is quadratic, C·(ε/εi*)², so the gap errors the market can hide from a pair trader form the ellipse (εA/εA*)² + (εB/εB*)² ≤ 1 — and the widest hidable gap is √(εA*² + εB*²): the attention floors combine in quadrature. (The hiding allocation sits inside each single-leg floor too, so single-leg correctors can't see it either.) Carry, by contrast, is a separable per-mana cost — a below-floor leg has negative marginal value at any size and can't be cross-subsidized — so carry floors add linearly. A skeptic who charges the pair trade two noticings scales the quadrature term by √2; that bound is quoted alongside the headline numbers where it matters.

How carry and attention combine (added Jul 12, when the page's verdicts moved to sum semantics). A corrector who pays for waiting will not trade a price past its carry edge εc — below that, holding the position has negative marginal value at any size — so on a gap g the claimable prize is the quadratic prize on the excess (g − εc), and covering one noticing cost requires g ≥ εc + εa: carry and attention add, exactly. The patient trader is the r = 0 slider setting, nothing more. Two deliberate non-additions: tick and attention combine as max, not sum — they are two consequences of the same min-worthwhile trade; and the 1% quote grid is not added as a flat floor — it acts as increments (the tie rule), and at high depth market orders land between grid points anyway, so stacking it on the bother floor would double-count.

Where the bands come from (the derivation)

A conditional market that refunds on N/A has a peculiar incentive structure, and it is computable. A trader who thinks the price is off by ε and bets faces two futures:

BranchProbabilityWhat happensCapital locked
Candidate not picked1 − pBfull refund (per Manifold's resolution code)~2 weeks — until Jul 27
Candidate pickedpBbet rides; edge ε pays off~4 months — until Nov 3

Setting expected profit against the cost of locked capital (rate r) gives the no-correction band — prices inside it are invisible to rational money:

ε* ≈ c · r · ( Tlive + TN/A · (1−pB)/pB ) + tick(pool)

Three things fall out. First, conditionality is nearly free for front-runners: because the N/A branch resolves in two weeks, the conditional-specific penalty (1−p_B)/p_B·T_NA only explodes below pB ≈ 0.15. The folk objection "thin conditional markets can't price anything" is really about longshot conditions with slow resolution. Second, the fee-free minimum bet (M$1 moves these pools ~0.1–0.2 points) means carry is the entire floor — the bands on the chart are the market's working precision. Third, a band is a statement about incentives, not freshness: prices can also simply be old. That failure mode needs its own check — the freshness audit in "Can these prices be trusted", which this market passes — and a price's evidential value decays with world-change since its last meaningful trade, not with today's pB. Sensitivity: ε* is linear in r; we quote r = 2%/month — halve or 2.5× the bands for 1% or 5%, or move the slider above the chart. The slider's 0.9% preset is the on-platform rate anchor: Manifold itself lends against positions at 0.03%/day ≈ 0.9%/month (margin loans) — a real borrowing rate available to any trader here, and the closest thing this economy has to a printed cost of capital.

The loans extension (added 2026-07-11, before publication; the frozen bands remain the no-loan case). Manifold's daily free loan returns ρ = 1% of a position's value per day, linearly, interest-free, repaid by deduction at resolution — including N/A resolution, which is why it is safe on the refund branch (in the resolution code the deduction extinguishes the loan — the holder keeps the loan cash, net whole in every world). Locked capital at day t is then max(0, 1 − ρt) of the stake, so a horizon of T days costs only

E(T) = T − ρT²/2 (for T ≤ 1/ρ; capped at 1/2ρ = 50 locked-day equivalents)

and the band becomes ε* ≈ c · r · ( E(Tlive) + E(TN/A) · (1−pB)/pB ). Two consequences: no Manifold position ever costs more than ~100 locked-day equivalents of carry, whatever its horizon; and because E compresses long horizons and spares short ones, loans shrink the live branch (×0.42 at four months) far more than the two-week refund branch (×0.93) — the front-runners' bands tighten ~2.2×, the tails' barely move. Caps (5% of equity per answer, 10%/day portfolio-wide) mean this discount belongs to diversified capital, not to a single concentrated corrector.
